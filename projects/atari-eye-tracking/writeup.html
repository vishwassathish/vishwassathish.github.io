Visualizing Gaze-Guided Attention in Atari

CSE 512 Data Visualization · Assignment 3 · University of Washington · Spring 2026

A. Project goal

Where do people look when they play Atari — and does that change as they get better? This project uses the Atari-HEAD dataset [1] to visualize human gaze behavior across six games, comparing casual players with high-score (expert) sessions.

The visualization has two complementary parts. The left side is a frame-by-frame player that shows exactly where each player was looking at every moment — with a raw gaze overlay, a foveated view that simulates limited peripheral vision, an integrated glimpse view showing which screen regions were recently sampled, and a gaze density heatmap. The right side presents aggregate statistics computed across all recorded trials for each game: how far eyes travel per minute, how often gaze stays focused in one place, and how much of the screen gets visited. These metrics are shown as time-series for the visible clip, as a pooled distribution over all trials, and as a normal-vs-expert comparison.

B. Dataset

The Atari-HEAD dataset [1] (Zenodo · arXiv 1903.06754) contains 117 hours of human gameplay across 20 Atari games, with eye-tracking data recorded at ~1000 Hz and co-registered to the 160x210 game frame coordinate space. Each trial file is a CSV with one row per frame: frame_id, episode_id, score, duration(ms), unclipped_reward, action, gaze_positions. Actions follow the standard ALE (Arcade Learning Environment) integer encoding.

Six games were selected to span a range of gameplay styles and reward structures:

  • Breakout: fast-paced, frequent rewards; ball-tracking demands predictive gaze.
  • Space Invaders: medium reward density; players tend to fixate on enemy rows rather than their own cannon.
  • Montezuma's Revenge: sparse rewards; long navigational stretches with broad exploratory gaze.
  • Ms. Pac-Man: maze navigation; gaze follows the ghost threat rather than pellets.
  • Hero: mixed structure with vertical movement; moderate scanning behavior.
  • Frostbite: platform-based; gaze concentrates on the jump trajectory ahead.

For each game, both normal and high-score (expert) sessions are included. Trial counts vary: 15–20 normal trials and 2–7 high-score trials per game (147 trials total).

C. Preprocessing

Raw trial files were processed by a Python script that performed the following steps (outputs committed to the repo):

  • Parse each CSV row; skip malformed lines.
  • Aggregate all gaze (x, y) samples within each frame into a list of points.
  • Compute a mean gaze position per frame for overlay rendering.
  • Select a contiguous segment of up to 1200 frames centered around high-reward-density periods to keep JSON payload size tractable.
  • Output one JSON per game/session: { "frames": [...], "rewards": [...] } with per-frame gaze arrays and reward timing.
  • Extract corresponding PNG frames from the dataset's frame archive into public/frames/<game>/.

The resulting JSON files (~0.8–1.1 MB each) are loaded lazily; frame images are fetched on demand and cached in a JavaScript Map to avoid re-request stalls during playback. A background preload pass fills the cache for smooth scrubbing.

D. Visualization design

The visualization is split into a frame player on the left and a gaze statistics panel on the right. All views are linked to the game selected in the Trial dropdown.

Panel 1: Game frame with gaze overlay

The raw game frame is drawn at 2× native resolution (320×420 canvas pixels) with a fading trail of the last 8 raw gaze samples — semi-transparent dots of decreasing size — a mean-position dot, and a white ring. The trail encodes both fixation precision and the direction of recent gaze motion. When the player scores a reward, a brief green badge fades in over the frame.

Panel 2 (tabs): Foveated view, Integrated glimpses, Gaze heatmap

These three views share the same canvas area and are toggled by the tab buttons.

Foveated view simulates limited peripheral vision: the frame is rendered with a sharp region centered on the current gaze point and a progressively blurred, darkened periphery. The blur is achieved by compositing a heavily downsampled-and-upsampled frame with a sharp radial patch masked by a Gaussian gradient. Three focus modes (Narrow / Medium / Wide) adjust the sharp-region radius. During rapid gaze movement (saccades), the sharp radius shrinks to reflect reduced visual acuity in transit [2, 3]. This view is intentionally approximate — its purpose is to make visible how little of a frame is processed at any instant.

Integrated glimpses show which screen regions have been sampled over the last 50 or 100 frames (~0.8–1.7 s). Motivated by Ballard et al.'s finding that people retrieve visual information just-in-time rather than maintaining detailed internal representations [4], this view draws sharp circular patches at each recent gaze position on a blurred background, with older fixations fading out. It reveals the spatial history of attention rather than just the current fixation.

Gaze heatmap accumulates a Gaussian density over the same window and renders it with a warm color ramp (black → deep red → orange → white). Useful for seeing where gaze clusters without frame content as a distraction.

Panel 3: Gaze statistics across all recorded trials

The right panel shows aggregate statistics computed from all raw trial files for the currently selected game. Three aggregation views are available:

  • Visible clip — time-series for the 60-second clip loaded in the player. The x-axis is time; lines or bars show how the metric accumulates or varies during that session.
  • All trials — each dot is one full play session (~4 minutes). All trials are pooled regardless of skill level. The diamond and error bar show mean ± 1 SD.
  • Normal vs high-score — same dots split by skill: left column is casual play, right column is best-recorded sessions. Useful for seeing how expert players look differently.

Three metrics are available for the aggregated views:

  • Gaze path (m/min) — total estimated eye movement per minute, calculated from consecutive mean-gaze positions mapped to the physical display dimensions (W = 0.646 m, H = 0.400 m). Higher values indicate more rapid scanning.
  • Focused gaze (%) — fraction of 1-second windows in which gaze stayed spatially compact. A window is "focused" if the median distance from each gaze point to the window's spatial median is below 8% of the screen diagonal. Higher = more sustained attention on a single region.
  • Screen coverage (%) — fraction of a 16×21 grid of screen cells visited by gaze over the full trial. Higher = broader spatial search.

Design note: Screen coverage is computed over the full trial rather than a sliding window because the question is about the player's overall spatial exploration strategy, not moment-to-moment sampling. Averaging sliding-window coverage underestimates total exploration and is harder to interpret.

Supporting controls

  • Timeline: A colour-coded bar shows reward density per 0.5 s bin (orange = high), giving an at-a-glance guide to interesting segments. Click or drag to seek.
  • Playback controls: Frame-by-frame stepping, reward-jump buttons (⏮ ⏭), and speed controls (0.5×, 1×, 2×) let users examine individual frames or quickly scan to reward events.

E. Observations from the visualization

  • Predictive gaze in Breakout: The foveated view makes visible that the player's sharp region tracks the ball trajectory and is often pre-positioned near bricks before contact. The integrated glimpse shows this as overlapping patches in the upper frame area accumulated over recent frames. The gaze-path metric for Breakout is among the highest across games, reflecting the continuous ball-tracking movement.
  • Target fixation in Space Invaders: Players fixate the nearest low-row alien rather than their own cannon — visible as the foveated sharp region hovering around enemy rows. Expert sessions show tighter, more spatially consistent fixation (higher focused-gaze %) with lower screen coverage, suggesting they monitor a smaller region more carefully.
  • Exploratory gaze in Montezuma's Revenge: The integrated glimpse shows broad coverage across much of the screen with no single dominant region, consistent with navigational search. Montezuma's Revenge has the highest screen-coverage values across all games, reflecting this search behavior.
  • Expert vs casual contrast: In the Normal vs high-score view, expert sessions tend toward higher focused-gaze % and lower screen coverage, consistent with the interpretation that skilled players have developed more efficient, targeted attention strategies — spending less time scanning and more time monitoring the task-critical region.

F. Design decisions

  • Dark color scheme: Chosen to match the Atari game aesthetic and avoid high-contrast backgrounds that would compete with the frame content. The same CSS theme applies to both the player and the statistics plots, which are generated as PNGs with matching background colors.
  • Tabbed perceptual views: Rather than showing three canvases simultaneously, tabs keep the layout compact and force the user to choose what they want to examine. The default (Foveated) is the most intuitive entry point for new users.
  • Static plots served as PNGs: The aggregate statistics involve parsing 147 full trial files (~tens of millions of gaze samples) — too expensive to compute client-side. Pre-generated PNGs keep the page lightweight and load instantly. A plot_manifest.json maps each game/aggregation/metric combination to the correct image path so the UI can switch plots without reloading.
  • All trials pooled (not split): The "All trials" view intentionally collapses normal and high-score sessions into a single distribution. Users who want to compare skill groups have the dedicated "Normal vs high-score" view. Conflating the two in a single colored strip plot was confusing when the legend was too small to read.
  • Screen coverage over full trial: Averaging per-second or per-window coverage underestimates exploration and is sensitive to how long a trial runs. Computing coverage as a single value over the full trial gives a cleaner measure of the player's overall spatial attention footprint.

Where do humans look when exploring for rewards?

CSE 512 Data Visualization · Assignment 3 · University of Washington · Spring 2026

A. Analysis question

How does a human player's visual attention relate to upcoming reward events in Atari games? Specifically, do players look at reward-relevant locations before scoring, suggesting predictive or goal-directed gaze, or does gaze shift only reactively? I further ask whether this pattern differs by game type: a fast, structured game with frequent rewards (Breakout, Space Invaders) vs. a sparse, explorative game where rewards are rare and spatially unpredictable (Montezuma's Revenge).

B. Dataset

I use the Atari-HEAD dataset (Zenodo · arXiv 1903.06754), a publicly released collection of human eye-tracking recordings during Atari game play. Each trial file is a CSV with one row per game frame containing: frame_id, episode_id, score, duration(ms), unclipped_reward, action, gaze_positions. Actions follow the standard ALE (Arcade Learning Environment) integer encoding. Gaze positions are raw (x, y) coordinate sequences sampled at ~1000 Hz and co-registered to the 160×210 game frame coordinate space.

I selected three games to span a range of reward structures:

  • Breakout — fast-paced; rewards occur every time the ball breaks a brick (multiple times per second at peak).
  • Space Invaders — medium reward density; rewards occur when a laser destroys an alien.
  • Montezuma's Revenge — reward-sparse; players must navigate through rooms collecting items; long stretches between any reward.

For each game I included both regular sessions and high-score (expert) sessions, giving six trial sets in total.

C. Preprocessing

Raw trial files were processed by a Python script (preprocess_breakout.py) that performed the following steps (run locally; outputs committed to the repo):

  • Parse each CSV row; skip malformed lines.
  • Aggregate all gaze (x, y) samples within a frame row into a list of points.
  • Compute a mean gaze position per frame for the overlay dot.
  • Select a contiguous segment of ≤ 1200 frames centred around high-reward-density periods to keep the JSON payload tractable for browser loading.
  • Output one JSON per game/session: { "frames": [...], "rewards": [...] } with per-frame gaze arrays and reward timing for use by the vis.
  • Extract the corresponding PNG frames from the dataset's frame archive and copy them into public/frames/<game>/.

The resulting JSON files (≈ 0.8–1.1 MB each) are loaded lazily by the JavaScript front-end; frame images are fetched on demand and cached to avoid re-request stalls during playback.

I chose to serve all assets statically so the visualization works without a server-side component — the entire site is plain HTML/CSS/JS deployable via GitLab Pages.

D. Design choices

The core design juxtaposes the raw game frame (with live gaze overlay) against a computed gaze heatmap, letting the viewer see both the moment-to-moment gaze trail and the integrated attention distribution across a time window.

  • Gaze overlay (left canvas): A fading trail of the last 8 raw gaze samples (semi-transparent cyan dots of decreasing size) plus a mean-position dot and a white ring encodes both precision and spread of fixation within a single frame. The trail shows direction of motion that a static dot cannot convey.
  • Heatmap (right canvas): Each gaze sample is smeared with an isotropic Gaussian (σ ≈ 3.5% of frame diagonal) and accumulated. Three modes expose different aggregation windows:
    • Window — rolling last N frames (10 or 30); good for local context.
    • Pre-reward — all gaze in a 2 s window before each reward event in the displayed segment; reveals the predictive pattern.
    • Pre-reward vs baseline — pre-reward heatmap minus a uniform-window baseline, highlighting locations that are over-attended before rewards.
  • Timeline: A colour-coded bar at the bottom shows reward density per 0.5 s bin (orange = high), giving an at-a-glance guide to interesting segments.
  • Playback controls: Frame-by-frame stepping and reward-jump buttons let users inspect individual frames or skip directly to reward events, supporting both exploratory browsing and focused analysis.
  • Dark theme: Matches the Atari game aesthetic and reduces interference with the colour-coded heatmap.

E. Findings

  • Predictive gaze in Breakout: In the pre-reward heatmap, attention concentrates in the brick-field 1–2 s before a break, well before the ball arrives. Players appear to track the ball trajectory and pre-position their gaze on the anticipated contact region. The diff heatmap shows this as a bright cluster in the upper two-thirds of the screen, clearly distinct from times without imminent rewards.
  • Threat tracking in Space Invaders: Pre-reward gaze clusters around the nearest low-row alien rather than the player cannon, suggesting players fixate the target rather than their weapon. The effect is most pronounced for expert-session players (high-score trials).
  • Exploratory gaze in Montezuma's Revenge: The pre-reward heatmap shows broad, diffuse attention spread across much of the screen with no single dominant cluster. This is consistent with navigational search: players must identify which path leads to the item, and fixation is distributed across multiple candidate locations. The transition from broad to focused gaze 0–0.5 s immediately before reward is visible as a small bright spot appearing in the diff mode.
  • Expert vs novice contrast: High-score sessions generally show tighter, more economical gaze patterns (smaller spread in the heatmap) than regular sessions, suggesting attentional efficiency as a correlate of skill.
